Exercise 4. Slowing Down a Dust Particle by Radiation Pressure at an Angle.

In the exercise above, Exercise 46.3.3 suppose light strikes the particle at \(30^\circ\) to the initial velocity as shown in the figure. What will be the particle’s velocity (direction and magnitude) after \(2\, \text{s}\text{?}\) Assume complete absorption.
Solution.
Once again, the radiation force on the particle is
\begin{equation*} F = \left( \dfrac{I}{c}\right) \: A,\ \ \dfrac{I}{c} = \dfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\:E_0^2. \end{equation*}
Putting in the numrical values we have
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{I}{c}= 238.7\:\textrm{W/m}^2,\ \ F = 9.55\times 10^{-10}\:\textrm{N}. \end{equation*}
Suppose the initial velocity be pointed towards the negative \(x\) axis and the light strike the particle at \(\theta = 30^{\circ}\) counterclockwise from the positive \(x\)-axis in the \(xy\)-plane. This gives the following for the \(x\)- and \(y\)-components for the acceleration.
\begin{equation*} a_x = \dfrac{F_x}{m} = \dfrac{F\:\cos\:\theta}{m},\ \ a_y = \dfrac{F_y}{m} = \dfrac{F\:\sin\:\theta}{m} \end{equation*}
Numerical values yield
\begin{equation*} a_x = 8.27\times 10^{-2}\:\textrm{m/s}^2,\ \ a_y = 4.77\times 10^{-2}\:\textrm{m/s}^2. \end{equation*}
Since \(\vec F\) is constant we will have a constant acceleration. The initial velocity has the following components here \(v_{0x} = -0.3\:\textrm{m/s}\) and \(v_y = 0\text{.}\) Therefore the velocity components at time \(t\) will be
\begin{equation*} v_x = v_{0x} + a_x\: t,\ \ v_y = v_{0y} + a_y\: t. \end{equation*}
Using the numerical values we obtain the components of the velocity at \(t=2\) sec are
\begin{align*} \amp v_x (\textrm {at } t = 2\:\textrm{s}) = -0.3+2\times 0.0827 = -0.135\:\textrm{m/s}, \\ \amp v_y (\textrm {at } t = 2\:\textrm{s}) =0+2\times 0.0477 = 0.0954\:\textrm{m/s}. \end{align*}
