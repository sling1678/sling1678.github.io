Example 4.49. Velocity and Acceleration from Position as Function of Time.

A car moves on a straight road such that its position from a reference point is given as \(x(t) = 3 + 5 t + 2 t^2 + 0.4 t^3,\ y=0,\ z=0\text{,}\) where \(t\) is in sec and \(x\) in meter. (a) Determine the velocity and acceleration at (i) \(t =0\text{,}\) (ii) \(t = 0.5\ \textrm{sec}\) and (iii) \(t = 1\ \textrm{sec}\text{.}\) (b) Determine the average velocity and average acceleration between \(t = 0\) and \(t = 1\ \textrm{sec}\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
Taking time derivative of \(x\) successively, we get \(v_x\) and \(a_x\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} \amp v_x = 5 + 4t+1.2 t^2;\ v_y = 0;\ v_z = 0\ \ \Longrightarrow \vec v = (5 + 4t+1.2 t^2)\ \hat u_x\ \ \textrm{[Unit: m/s]}\\ \amp a_x = 4+2.4 t;\ a_y = 0;\ a_z = 0\ \ \Longrightarrow \vec a = (4+2.4 t)\ \hat u_x\ \ \textrm{[Unit: m/s}^2\text{]} \end{align*}
Put the values of \(t\) for the instants in question. For instance, when you put (iii) \(t=1\) you get
\begin{equation*} v_x = 10.2\,\text{m/s},\ \ a_x = 6.4\,\text{m/s}^2. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. b
The \(x\)-components of average velocity and average acceleration between \(t=0\) and \(t=1\) s are
\begin{align*} \amp v_{av,x} = \frac{x(1\ \textrm{s})-x(0)}{1\ \textrm{s}-0} = \frac{10.4\ \textrm{m} - 3\ \textrm{m}}{1\ \textrm{s}} = 7.4\ \textrm{m/s}\\ \amp a_{av,x} = \frac{v_x(1\ \textrm{s})-v_x(0)}{1\ \textrm{s}-0} = \frac{10.2\ \textrm{m/s} - 5\ \textrm{m/s}}{1\ \textrm{s}} = 5.2\ \textrm{m/s}^2 \end{align*}
Since the \(y\)- and \(z\)-components are zero here, the magnitudes of the average velocity and average acceleration are equal to the absolute value of their \(x\)-components, and since the \(x\)-components are positive, the directions of these physical quantities are towards the positive \(x\)-axis.
