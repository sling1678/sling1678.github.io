Example 39.22. Power Dissipation in an \(RL\) Circuit.

A motor of self-inductance \(\mathcal{L} = 10\,\text{H}\) shown as coil in FigureÂ 39.23 is connected in a circuit with a resistor of resistance \(R_1=200.0\,\Omega\text{,}\) a dissipative resitor \(R_2=1000.0\,\Omega\) and a battery of voltage \(V=9.0\,\text{V}\) using a switch. In the ON position, switch connects \(R_1\) and battery to the motor and in the OFF position, only \(R_2\) is connected to the motor.
(a) The switch is in the ON position for a long time. (i) What is the time constant of this circuit? (ii) How much current flows at the peak? (iii) How much energy is stored in this circuit at the peak current? (b) The switch is now thrown into the OFF position. (i) What is the time constant of this circuit? (ii) How long will it take for half of the energy to be dissipated in this circuit?
Solution 1. a
(i) The time constant is easily worked out to be \(\mathcal{L}/R_1\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \tau_1 = \frac{\mathcal{L}}{R_1} = \frac{10}{200} = \frac{1}{20}\,\text{sec}. \end{equation*}
(ii) The peak current is when all voltage of the battery falls across the resistor \(R_1\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} I_\text{max} = \frac{V}{R_1} = \frac{9}{200}\,\text{A}. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. b
(i) Now, the active circuit has only \(\mathcal{L}\) and \(R_2\text{.}\) Hence, the time constant is
\begin{equation*} \tau_2 = \frac{\mathcal{L}}{R_2} = \frac{10}{1000} = \frac{1}{100}\,\text{sec}. \end{equation*}
(ii) The time constant for power dissipation is twice the time constant.
\begin{equation*} U(t) = U_0\, e^{-t/(\tau_2/2)}. \end{equation*}
We need \(t\) when \(U(t)=\frac{1}{2}\,U_0\text{.}\) Therefore,
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2} = e^{-t/(\tau_2/2)}. \end{equation*}
Taking natural log of both sides we get
\begin{equation*} t = \frac{\tau_2}{2}\,\ln\,2 = 3.47\,\text{msec} \end{equation*}
