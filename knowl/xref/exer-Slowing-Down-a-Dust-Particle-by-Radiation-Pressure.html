Exercise 3. Slowing Down a Dust Particle by Radiation Pressure.

A microscopic spherical dust particle of radius \(2\,\mu\text{m}\) and mass \(10\,\mu\text{g}\) is moving in the outer space at a constant speed of \(30\, \text{cm/sec}\text{.}\) A wave of light strikes the particle from the opposite direction of its motion, and gets absorbed. Assuming the particle decelerates uniformly to zero speed in one second, what is the average electric field amplitude in the light?
Answer.
\(7.35 \times 10^6\, \text{N/C}\text{.}\)
Solution.
Since radiation force on the particle will be equal to the product of the radiation pressure \(P = \dfrac{I}{c}\) and the area of cross-section \(A\) the particle in the path of the radiation. Therefore the acceleration \(a\) of the particle will be
\begin{equation*} a = \dfrac{1}{m}\: \left( \dfrac{I}{c} \right)\: A.\ \ \ \ \ \ \ (1) \end{equation*}
From the given data we have
\begin{equation*} a = \dfrac{v_0-0}{\Delta t}.\ \ \ \ \ \ \ (2) \end{equation*}
From (1) and (2)
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{I}{c} = \dfrac{mv_0}{A\Delta t}. \end{equation*}
But, for a plane electromagnetic wave, \(I/c\) is also equal to \(\dfrac{1}{2}\:\epsilon_0:E_0^2\text{.}\) Therefore,
\begin{equation*} E_0 = \sqrt{\dfrac{2mv_0}{A\Delta t\epsilon_0}} = 7.35\times 10^6\:\textrm{N/C}. \end{equation*}
