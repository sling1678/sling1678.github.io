Exercise 2. (Calculus) Speed and Distance of a Skier with Inertial Drag Force.

A \(70\text{-kg}\) skier glides down a “frictionless” slope starting from rest on a flat incline with angle of inclination \(\theta=30^\circ\text{.}\) The motion of skier is impeded by inertial drag force from air. Suppose \(C_D=0.4\) and \(\rho_\text{air}=1.22\text{kg /m}^3\text{,}\) and the skier presents and area of \(A=1.2\text{ m}^2\text{.}\) (a) Find the terminal speed of the skier. (b) Find the expression of speed \(v\) as a function of time \(t\) with \(t=0\) at start where \(v=0\text{.}\) (c) Find the expression of distance on the slope the skier travels in time \(t\text{.}\)
Hint.
(a) Set up the condition for zero acceleration. (b) Write \(F_x = m dv_x/dt\) and solve for \(v_x\text{.}\) (c) Integrate the result of (b).
Answer.
(a) \(34.2\text{ m/s}\text{,}\) (b) \((34.3\text{ m/s})\; \tanh( 0.143\; t)\text{,}\) (c) \((239\text{ m}) \ln( \cosh( 0.143\; t ) )\text{.}\) You might different expressions for (b) and (c) since the integrals can be written in other forms also.
Solution 1. (a)
(a) Figure 6.67 shows three forces acting on the skier. Let \(v_x\) and \(a_x = dv_x/dt\) denote the \(x\) component of velocity and acceleration respectively. Equation of motion \(F_x = m a_x= m dv_x/dt\) will be
\begin{equation} m \frac{dv_x}{dt} = m g \sin\theta - \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_x^2.\tag{6.27} \end{equation}
When speed is terminal speed \(v_t\text{,}\) then, it has zero acceleration. This gives
\begin{equation*} v_t = \sqrt{ \frac{2 m g \sin\theta}{ C_D \rho A } }. \end{equation*}
Putting the numerical values we get
\begin{equation*} v_t = \sqrt{ \frac{2 \times 70 \times 9.81 \times \sin\, 30^\circ}{ 0.4 \times 1.22 \times 1.2 } } = 34.2\text{ m/s}. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. (b)
Now, we work with the equation of motion, Eq. (6.27).
\begin{equation*} m \frac{dv_x}{dt} = m g \sin\theta - \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_x^2. \end{equation*}
Let’s divide by \(m\) and replace myriad constants by symbols.
\begin{equation} \frac{dv_x}{dt} = a - b v_x^2,\tag{6.28} \end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*} a = g \sin\theta,\ \ \ b = \frac{1}{2m} C_D \rho A. \end{equation*}
Note that \(v_t = \sqrt{a/b}\text{.}\) Rearrange Eq. (6.28) and integrate from \(t=0\) to \(t\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \int_0^v \frac{d v_x}{ a - b v_x^2 } = \int_0^t dt. \end{equation*}
This gives (with \(v_t = \sqrt{a/b}\)):
\begin{equation} v = v_t \tanh(b v_t t ).\tag{6.29} \end{equation}
Now, using the numerical values we get
\begin{equation*} v = (34.3\text{ m/s})\; \tanh( 0.143\; t). \end{equation*}
Solution 3. (c)
Now, we integrate Eq. (6.29) to get the distance \(\Delta x\) on the slope.
\begin{equation*} \Delta x = \int_0^t v dt = \int_0^t v_t \tanh(b v_t t ) dt. \end{equation*}
This gives
\begin{equation*} \Delta x = \frac{1}{b} \log( \cosh( b v_t t ) ). \end{equation*}
Now, using the numerical values we get
\begin{equation*} \Delta x = (239\text{ m}) \ln( \cosh( 0.143\; t ) ). \end{equation*}
