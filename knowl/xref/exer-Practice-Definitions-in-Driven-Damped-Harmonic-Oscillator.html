Exercise 5. Practice Definitions in Driven Damped Harmonic Oscillator.

Consider a one-dimensional damped oscillator of mass \(m = 0.5\) kg, \(\omega_0\) = 10 rad/sec, and \(\beta= 1\) rad/sec. Suppose the oscillator can oscillate along the \(x\)-axis. A sinusoidal force with the \(x\) component \(F_x = 100\ \textrm{N}\cos(\omega t)\) acts on the oscillator.
  1. What should be the condition on time \(t\) so that the steady state for the oscillator can be assumed?
  2. Suppose the steady state has been reached and the displacement of the oscillator can be written as \(x = A_c \cos(\omega t) + A_s \sin(\omega t)\text{.}\) Find the amplitudes \(A_c\) and \(A_s\) as a function of driving frequency \(\omega\text{.}\)
  3. Find the amplitude of the steady sate oscillations.
  4. Find the phase lag?
  5. What is the expression for the instantaneous power of the driving force?
  6. Find the average power as a function of driving frequency.
  7. At what frequency is the power maximum?
  8. At what frequency is the amplitude of the oscillatorâ€™s motion maximum?
  9. What is the width at half height of the power versus driving frequency plot?
Solution.
Let us start with listing the given quantities.
\begin{equation*} m = 0.5\,\text{kg},\ \ \omega_0 = 10\,\text{rad/sec},\ \ \beta = 1.0\,\text{rad/sec},\ \ F_0 = 100\,\text{N}. \end{equation*}
  1. Steady state when \(t>>1/\beta\text{.}\) This means that we need to wait for \(t>>1\) sec before assuming the steady state condition for the given oscillator.
  2. Left as an exercise for the student.
  3. Amplitude of the steady state solution is related to the \(A_c\) and \(A_s\) and has been given in the textbook.
    \begin{equation*} A = \frac{D}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 +(2\beta\omega)^2}}. \end{equation*}
    Here \(D = F_0/m\text{.}\)
  4. The phase lag \(\delta\) will be given by
    \begin{equation*} \tan\delta = \frac{2\beta\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}. \end{equation*}
  5. The instantaneous power of the driving force is
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = \vec F_d \cdot \vec v = F_x v_x. \end{equation*}
    With \(x = A\cos(\omega t-\delta)\) and \(F_x = F_0\cos(\omega t)\) we get
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = -\omega A F_0 \cos(\omega t)\cos(\omega t-\delta). \end{equation*}
    If you used \(x(t) = A_c \cos(\omega t) + A_s \sin(\omega t)\text{,}\) then you would get
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = \omega F_0 \cos(\omega t)\left[ -A_c\sin(\omega t) + A_s\cos(\omega t) \right]. \end{equation*}
  6. The average power will be
    \begin{equation*} P_{\textrm{ave}} = \frac{1}{2}\omega F_0 A_s. \end{equation*}
    In terms of the formula using the amplitude \(A\) we get
    \begin{equation*} P_{\textrm{ave}} = \frac{1}{2}\omega F_0 A\sin\delta. \end{equation*}
  7. Power is maximum at its resonance frequency, which occurs at \(\omega = \omega_0\text{.}\)
  8. The maximum amplitude occurs at its resonance frequency which occurs at \(\omega = \sqrt{\omega_0^2-2\beta^2}\text{.}\)
