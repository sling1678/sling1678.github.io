Example 10.15. Angular Momentum of a Dumbbell About Arbitrary Axis.

Figure 10.16 shows a dumbbell of two point masses \(m\) and length \(2a\) that rotates about an axis at angular speed \(\omega\) that is not a principal axis of the dumbbell. Pointing \(z\)-axis towards the axis of rotation leaves the dumbell rotating at an angle \(\theta\) with respect to the axis.
Supposing that the masses are in \(xz\)-plane at time \(t=0\) so that azimuth of one of the masses is \(\phi=\omega t\text{.}\) (a) Find angular momentum vector at an arbitrary instant. (b) Show that the magnitude of angular momentum is constant in time. Ignore the mass of the connecting rod.
Answer.
(b) \(2m\omega a^2\sin\theta\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
First we find moments of inertia using coordinates of the two masses and then pick a moment in time to find \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates in terms of spherical angles.
Let’s work out coordinates of the two masses at an instant when the azimuth angle for one of the masses is \(\phi=\omega t\) as shown in Figure 10.17. From this figure, we can work out following coordinates of the two masses.
\begin{align*} \amp x_1 = a\,\sin\theta\cos\phi,\ y_1 = a\,\sin\theta\sin\phi,\ z_1 = -a\cos\theta\\ \amp x_2 = -x_1,\ y_2 = -y_1,\ z_2 = -z_1 \end{align*}
Now, we compute the six moments of inertia for this system.
\begin{align*} \amp I_{xx} = 2ma^2(\sin^2\theta \sin^2\phi + \cos^2\theta) \\ \amp I_{yy} = 2ma^2(\sin^2\theta \cos^2\phi + \cos^2\theta) \\ \amp I_{zz} = 2ma^2(\sin^2\theta \cos^2\phi + \sin^2\theta \sin^2\phi ) = 2ma^2\sin^2\theta \\ \amp I_{xy} = -2ma^2\sin^2\theta \sin\phi\cos\phi\\ \amp I_{xz} = 2ma^2\sin\theta\cos\theta \cos\phi \\ \amp I_{yz} = 2ma^2\sin\theta\cos\theta \sin\phi \end{align*}
Since angular velocity is pointed towards \(z\)-axis, we have
\begin{equation*} \omega_x=0,\ \ \omega_y=0,\ \ \omega_z = \omega. \end{equation*}
Therefore, after using \(\phi=\omega t\text{,}\) Cartesian components of angular momentum will be
\begin{align*} \amp L_x = I_{xz} \omega_z = 2m\omega a^2\sin\theta\cos\theta \cos\omega t\\ \amp L_y = I_{yz} \omega_z = 2m\omega a^2\sin\theta\cos\theta \sin\omega t\\ \amp L_z = I_{zz}\omega_z = 2ma^2\omega\sin^2\theta \end{align*}
Note that even though \(L_x\) and \(L_y\) are time-dependent, total angular momentum is conserved as seen by \(L^2\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} L^2 \amp = L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2 \\ \amp = (2m\omega a^2\sin\theta\cos\theta)^2 \left(\cos^2\omega t + \sin^2\omega t\right) + (2ma^2\omega\sin^2\theta)^2 \\ \amp = (2m\omega a^2\sin\theta\cos\theta)^2 + (2ma^2\omega\sin^2\theta)^2. \end{align*}
Of course, you can simplify it even more, but you can already see that \(L^2\) is constant in time.
Solution 2. b
From the components of angular momentum vector we get the magnitude by
\begin{equation*} L = \sqrt{L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2}. \end{equation*}
Let’s work out each square and then add them.
\begin{align*} \amp L_x^2 = (2m\omega a^2)^2 \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta \cos^2\phi\\ \amp L_y^2 = (2m\omega a^2)^2 \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta \sin^2\phi \\ \amp L_z^2 = (2m\omega a^2)^2 \sin^4\theta \end{align*}
Adding the first two we get
\begin{equation*} L_x^2 + L_y^2 = (2m\omega a^2)^2 \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta. \end{equation*}
Adding this to \(L_z^2\) involves following algebra step.
\begin{equation*} \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta + \sin^4\theta = \sin^2\theta. \end{equation*}
Therefore, magnitude of angular momentum will be
\begin{equation*} L = 2m\omega a^2\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
Since we do not have \(\phi\) in the magnitude, it is constant in time.
