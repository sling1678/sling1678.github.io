Exercise 1. Energy Dissipated in Charging a Capacitor in an RC Circuit.

A 200 pF capacitor is connected in series to a \(2\, \text{k}\Omega\) resistor, a \(12\, \text{V}\) battery and a switch. (a) Determine the time it will take to get the capacitor \(50\%\) charged. (b) Determine the maximum charge on the capacitor if allowed to charge for a long time. (c) Determine energy lost during the charging process.
Answer.
(a) \(2.8\times 10^{-7}\ \text{s}\text{,}\) (b) \(2.4\times 10^{-9}\text{,}\) (c) \(2.9\times 10^{-8}\ \text{J}\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
For the present circuit, we have a complete solution giving charge on the capacitor.
\begin{equation*} Q(t) = C\:V\:\left(1 - e^{t/\tau} \right)\ \ \ \ (1) \end{equation*}
where the time constant \(\tau = RC\text{,}\) and \(CV\) is the charge on the capacitor when fully charged. The time constant here is
\begin{equation*} \tau = RC = 2000\:\Omega\times 200\:\text{pF} = 600\: \text{ms}. \end{equation*}
\noindent (a) We use Eq (1) to set up an equation for \(t\text{.}\) The question becomes, find t when
\begin{equation*} 0.5 = 1 - e^{t/\tau}. \end{equation*}
To solve this equation for \(t\) we will separate the exponetial on one side of the equation and take natural logarithm of both sides.
\begin{equation*} t = -\tau\:\ln(0.5) = -0.4\:\mu\text{s}\times \ln(0.5) = 277\: \text{ms}. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. b
At full charge, the potential difference across the plate will be \(12\:\text{V}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} Q_{\text{max}} = C\:V = 200\:\text{pF} \times 12\:\text{V} = 2.4\:\text{nC}. \end{equation*}
Solution 3. c
The energy is lost in the resistorâ€™s heating. The rate of energy deposited in the resistor is given by the power dissipated, \(P = I^2 R\text{,}\) where current is time derivative of charge on the capacitor given in equation (1). The total energy dissipated is obtained by integrating \(P\) from t=0 to \(\infty\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \text{Energy dissipated} = \int_0^{\infty}\:P(t) dt = \int_0^{\infty}\:I^2 R dt = \frac{R}{2\tau}\:C^2 V^2 = \frac{1}{2}Q_{\text{max}}V. \end{equation*}
Putting in the numerical values, we obtain the following.
\begin{equation*} \text{Energy dissipated} = \frac{1}{2}\times 2.4\:\text{nC}\times 12\:\text{V} = 288\:\mu\text{J}. \end{equation*}
