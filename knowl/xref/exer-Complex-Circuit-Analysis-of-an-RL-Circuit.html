Exercise 4. Complex Circuit Analysis of an RL Circuit.

A \(1.5\text{-k}\Omega\) resistor and \(30\text{-mH}\) inductor are connected in series across a \(120\text{-V}\) (rms ) AC power source oscillating at \(60\text{-Hz}\) frequency.
  1. Find the current in the circuit.
  2. Find the voltage drops across the resistor and inductor.
  3. Find the impedance of the circuit.
  4. Find the power dissipated in the resistor.
  5. Find the power dissipated in the inductor.
  6. Find the power produced by the source.
Answer.
(a) \((0.113\, \text{A})\, \cos(2\pi\times 60 t - 0.0075)\) when \(V(t) = (120\sqrt{2}\, \text{V}) \cos(2\pi\times 60 t)\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
The source voltage is given to be \(V(t) = V_0\:\cos(\omega t)\text{,}\) with \(V_0 = 120\sqrt{2} = 169.7\:\textrm{V}\) and \(\omega = 2\pi\times 60 = 120\pi\:\textrm{sec}^{-1}\text{.}\) In complex notation this is replaced by \(V_0\:e^{i\omega t}\text{.}\) Now, we divide this by complex impedance to find the complex current. The complex impedance here is
\begin{equation*} Z = R + i\omega L = |Z|\: e^{i\phi},\quad\quad (1) \end{equation*}
where
\begin{align*} \amp |Z| = \left( R^2 + \omega^2 L^2 \right)^{1/2} = 1500\:\Omega,\\ \amp \phi = \tan^{-1}\left( \dfrac{\omega L}{ R }\right)= 0.0075\:\textrm{rad} \end{align*}
Therefore, the complex current is
\begin{equation*} I = \dfrac{V}{Z} = \dfrac{V_0}{|Z|}\: e^{i(\omega t -\phi)} = (0.113\:\textrm{A})\:e^{i(\omega t -0.0075)}. \end{equation*}
The real part of this complex current gives the current in the circuit.
\begin{equation*} I = (0.113\:\textrm{A})\:\cos(\omega t -0.0075). \end{equation*}
Therefore, the amplitude of current is \(0.113\:\textrm{A}\) and the phase constant \(0.0.0075\, \text{rad}\) or \(0.4^{\circ}\) behind the phase of the source voltage.
Solution 2. b
Now, we can use the current to find the voltage drop across the resistor.
\begin{equation*} V_R = (0.113\:\textrm{A}\times 1500\:\Omega)\:\cos(\omega t -0.0075) = (169\:\textrm{V})\:\cos(\omega t -0.0075). \end{equation*}
The phase of the voltage across the inductor will be ahead of the phase of the current by \(\pi/2\text{.}\) The amplitude of the voltage across the inductor will be equal to the product of the amplitude of the current and the reactance of the inductor, which is \(X_L = \omega L = 11.3\:\Omega\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} V_L = (0.113\:\textrm{A}\times 11.3\:\Omega)\:\cos(\omega t -0.0075+\frac{\pi}{2}) = (1.28\:\textrm{V})\:\cos(\omega t -0.0075+\frac{\pi}{2}). \end{equation*}
Solution 3. c
See (1) in part (a).
Solution 4. d
\begin{equation*} P_R = \dfrac{1}{2}I_0^2 R = \dfrac{1}{2}\times (0.113\:\textrm{A})^2\times 1500\:\Omega = 9.6\:\textrm{W}. \end{equation*}
Solution 5. e
No power is dissipated in the inductor since current through the inductor and the voltage across the inductor are \(\pi/2\) rad out phase.
Solution 6. f
This will equal the power dissipated in the resistor: \(9.6\, \text{W}\text{.}\)
