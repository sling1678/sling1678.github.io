Example 34.24. Resistance in Filaments of a 60-W Light Bulb.

(a) How much is the resistance in the filaments of a 60-W light bulb that operates at a voltage of 220 volts?
(b) If in 10 seconds bulb emits 200 J of energy in light, what is its efficiency?
(c) Supposing light is produced with the same efficiency when you use the bulb in a 110-V line, how much light energy will be generated in 10 seconds?
Answer.
(a) \(810 \Omega\text{,}\) (b) \(33.3\%\text{,}\) (c) \(50\text{ J}\text{.}\)
Solution 1. (a)
The bulb uses energy at a rate of \(60\text{ W}\) at \(\Delta\phi = 220\text{ V}\text{.}\) Using the formula for power dissipation in terms of potential difference and resistance we find
\begin{equation*} R = \frac{V^2}{P}. \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} R = = \frac{(220\:\text{V})^2}{60\:\text{W}} = 810\:\Omega. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. (b)
The energy emitted in desired form in 10 seconds = \(200 \text{J}\) while the energy used in 10 seconds \(= 60 \text{ W} \times 10 \text{ s} = 600 \text{ J}\text{.}\) Therefore, the efficiency is
\begin{equation*} \eta = \frac{200 \text{ J}}{600 \text{ J} } = 1/3 = 33.3\%. \end{equation*}
Solution 3. (c)
With \(R = 810\:\Omega\) and \(\Delta\phi=110\text{ V}\text{,}\) the power used will be
\begin{equation*} P = \frac{V^2}{R} = 14.9\text{ W}. \end{equation*}
With 33.3% efficiency, the light energy in 10 seonds will be
\begin{equation*} E = \eta P \Delta t = 0.333 \times 14.9\text{ W} \times 10\text{ s} = 50\text{ J}. \end{equation*}
Note that the light energy is not 1/2. Actually, it is 1/4. You could make the arguments based on \(P \sim (\Delta\phi)^2\text{.}\) Can you see how?
