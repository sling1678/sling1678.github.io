Checkpoint 50.27. Photocurrents from Zinc.

In a photoelectric experiment on zinc a monochromatic light of wavelength 250 nm of intensity 2 \(\mu\)W/cm\(^2\) is incident on 1.2 cm\(^2\) area. What is the maximum possible photocurrent if the work function of zinc is 4.31 eV?
Hint.
Maximum possible current when each photon causes ejection of an electron.
Answer.
\(4.8\times 10^{-7}\:\textrm{A}\text{.}\)
Solution.
To calculate the maximum possible current we will assume that each photon leads to the release of one electron. This will give us the maximum number \((N)\) of electron released per unit time. Each electron carries a charge \(e\text{,}\) therefore the electric current coresponding to these electrons will be equal to \(Ne\text{.}\) Energy deposited by photons per second will be
\begin{equation*} E = IA = 2\times 10^{-6}\: \textrm{W/m}^2 \times 1.2\:\textrm{cm}^2 = 2.4\times 10^{-6}\: \textrm{W}. \end{equation*}
Each photon carries energy
\begin{equation*} E_\gamma = h f = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}\:\textrm{J.s}\times 3\times 10^{8}\:\textrm{m/s}}{250\times 10^{-9}\:\textrm{m}} = 7.96\times 10^{-19}\:\textrm{J}. \end{equation*}
Therefore, the number of photons striking the zinc surface, which we have assumed to equal the maximum number of electrons released per second.
\begin{equation*} N = \frac{E}{E_\gamma} = \frac{2.4\times 10^{-6}\: \textrm{W}}{7.96\times 10^{-19}\:\textrm{J}} = 3\times 10^{12}\:\textrm{s}^{-1}. \end{equation*}
Each electron carries a charge of \(e = 1.6\times 10^{-19}\) C. Therefore, the total electric current per unit time flowing
\begin{equation*} i = Ne = 4.8\times 10^{-7}\:\textrm{A}. \end{equation*}
