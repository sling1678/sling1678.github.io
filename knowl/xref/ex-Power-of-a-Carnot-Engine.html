Exercise 2. Power of a Carnot Engine.

A Carnot engine working between two heat baths of temperatures \(600\text{K}\) and \(273\text{K}\) completes each cycle in \(5\text{ sec}\text{.}\) In each cycle, the engine absorbs \(10\text{ kJ}\) of heat. Find the power of the engine.
Hint.
Find \(\eta\) from temperatures and then use it to get \(W\) and \(P=W/\Delta t\text{.}\)
Answer.
\(2,400\text{ W}\text{.}\)
Solution.
The power \(P\) of the engine will be work \(W\) divided by time \(\Delta t\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} P = \frac{W}{\Delta t}. \end{equation*}
From the definition of efficiency we can obtain \(W\) from the heat rejected to the lower temperature bath and the efficiency as follows.
\begin{equation*} \eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{W}{W-Q_C}\ \ \Longrightarrow\ \ W = \left( \frac{\eta}{1-\eta}\right)\ Q_C. \end{equation*}
The efficiency here is equal to the efficiency of the Carnot cycle.
\begin{equation*} \eta = 1- \frac{T_C}{T_H}\ \ \Longrightarrow\ \ \frac{\eta}{1-\eta} = \frac{T_H-T_C}{T_C}. \end{equation*}
Putting in the values here
\begin{equation*} \frac{\eta}{1-\eta} = \frac{600-273}{273} = 1.2. \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} W = 1.2\times\ Q_C = 1.2\times 10\ \text{kJ} = 12\ \text{kJ}. \end{equation*}
This work is done in 5 sec. Therefore, the power is
\begin{equation*} P = \frac{W}{\Delta t} = \frac{12\ \text{kJ}}{5\ s} = 2400\ \text{W}. \end{equation*}
