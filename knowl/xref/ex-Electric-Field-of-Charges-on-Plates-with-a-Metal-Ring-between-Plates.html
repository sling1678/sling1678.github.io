Example 32.21. Electric Field of Charges on Plates with a Metal Ring between Plates.

An uncharged metallic ring is placed between two charged parallel plates of a parallel plate capacitor with charges \(\pm Q\) as shown in Figure 32.22. Draw electric field lines and equipotential surfaces.
Answer.
See the solution.
Solution.
Recall the rules:
  1. Electric field lines begin at positive charge and end at negative charges.
  2. Electric field lines land perpendicularly on metals.
Figure 32.23 shows the electric field lines and equipotential lines (dashed).
You may remember that when there is no metal ring between plates, electric field lines just go straight from positive plate to the negative plate. Now, since metal ring will be polarized, field lines from positive plate will bend to land on the negative side of the polarization of the ring. Similarly, on the other side of the ring, which has positive induced charges, field lines will start which will land on the negative plate. Far away from the ring, the influence of induced charges will die down and field lines will be similar to the case when there was no ring.
Once we have drawn the electric field lines, we can draw equipotential lines that are pependicular to the E-field lines. As shown, they will be be closed curves near the ring and bowl-like as you get further from the ring and towards the plates.
