Exercise 3. (Calculus) Numerical Study of Current Rise and Magnetic Energy Storage in a Coil.

A coil of resistance \(0.2\,\Omega\) and inductance \(0.4\, \text{mH}\) is connected across a battery of voltage \(12\,\text{V}\) and internal resistance \(0.01\,\Omega\) and a switch S. The coil has radius \(5\, \text{cm}\) and length \(30\, \text{cm}\text{.}\) The switch is closed at \(t =0\text{.}\)
(a) Find the time it will take the current to reach \(99\%\) of its final value. (b) Find the amount of energy transferred to the coils as heat during that time? (c) How much energy is stored in the coils as magnetic field at \(t=4\,\text{msec}\text{?}\) (d) (Calculus) At what rate is the current increasing at \(t = 4\, \text{msec}\text{?}\)
Hint.
(a) Use series \(RL\) circuit equations. (b) Resistance of coils is important here. (c) Use magnetic energy formula. (d) Need \(dI/dt\) - you can use solution \(I(t)\) and take derivative.
Answer.
(a) \(9.2 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{sec}\text{,}\) (b) 0.6 J, (c) 0.54 J, (d) \(4.1 \times 10^3\ \text{A/s}\text{.}\)
Solution 1. (a)
Recall the solution of a series LR-circuit is
\begin{equation*} I(t) = I_{\text{max}}\:\left(1 - e^t/\tau \right), \ \ \ \ \ \ (1) \end{equation*}
with
\begin{equation*} I_{\text{max}} = \dfrac{V}{R},\ \ \tau = \dfrac{L}{R}. \end{equation*}
Setting \(I = 0.99\; I_{\text{max}}\) in Eq. (1) gives
\begin{equation*} 0.99 = 1 - e^{-t/\tau}. \end{equation*}
Solving this equation for \(t\) we find
\begin{align*} t \amp = \tau \ln(100) \\ \amp = \dfrac{0.4\times 10^{-3}\:\text{H}}{0.2\:\Omega}\:\ln(100) = 9.2\times 10^{-4}\:\text{s}. \end{align*}
For use in the second part of this exercise let us denote this time by letter \(T\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} T \equiv 9.2\times 10^{-4}\:\text{s}. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. (b)
The energy spent in this time can be obtained by integrating the instantaneous power.
\begin{align*} W \amp = \int P dt = \int I^2 R dt\\ \amp = \dfrac{V^2}{R}\:\int_0^T\: \left( 1 - 2 e^{-Rt/L} + e^{-2Rt/L} \right)\: dt. \end{align*}
The integrals are easy to do and the result comes out to be
\begin{equation*} W = \dfrac{V^2}{R}\left[ T + \dfrac{2L}{R}\left( e^{-RT/L} -1 \right) -\dfrac{L}{2R} \left( e^{-2RT/L} -1 \right) \right]. \end{equation*}
Putting in the numerical values we find \(W = 0.6\:\text{J}\text{.}\)
Solution 3. (c)
Here
\begin{equation*} I(\text{at }4\:\text{ms}) = \dfrac{12}{0.2}\left(1 - e^{-500\times 0.004}\right) = 52\:\text{A}. \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{align*} U \amp = \dfrac{1}{2}LI^2\\ \amp = \dfrac{1}{2}\times 0.4\times 10^{-3} \times 52^2 = 0.54\:\text{J}. \end{align*}
Solution 4. (d)
A direct calculation on (1) yields
\begin{equation*} \frac{dI}{dt} =\frac{V}{R} \frac{R}{L}\: e^{-Rt/L}. \end{equation*}
Putting in the numerical values gives \(4.1\times 10^3\: \text{A/s}\text{.}\)
