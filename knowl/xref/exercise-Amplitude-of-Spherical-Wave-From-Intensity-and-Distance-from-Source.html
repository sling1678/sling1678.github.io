Example 14.29. Amplitude of Spherical Wave From Intensity and Distance from Source.

A spherical mechanical wave traveling in salt water of density \(1050\text{ kg/m}^3\) is given by the following function.
\begin{equation*} \psi(x,y,z,t) = \frac{A}{r} \cos\left(10\, r - 200\, t \right), \end{equation*}
where \(r\) is in \(\text{m}\text{,}\) \(t\) in \(\text{sec}\) and \(\psi\) in \(\text{m}\text{.}\) Here \(r\) is spherical radial coordinate from the source at the origin. The intensity of the wave at \(r=0.25\text{ m}\) from the origin is \(120\text{ W/m}^2\text{.}\)
Find the value of constant \(A\) in the wave function.
Answer.
\(7.54\times 10^{-5}\text{ m}^2\text{.}\)
Solution.
We will use \(I = \frac{2 \pi^2 \mu \lambda f^3 D^2}{A_\perp}\) with \(D=A/r\) to find the value of \(A\text{.}\) We note that the wave will pass through a spherical surface of radius \(r\text{.}\) Hence,
\begin{equation*} A_\perp = 4\pi r^2. \end{equation*}
Therefore, we have the following for intensity at \(r\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} I = \frac{\pi \mu \lambda f^3 A^2}{2 r^4}. \end{equation*}
By equating mass in \(r\) to \(r+\Delta r\) in two ways, one using linear density \(\mu\Delta r\) and another by regular density \(\rho \times A\Delta r\) with \(A=4\pi r^2\text{,}\) we get
\begin{equation*} \mu = 4\pi r^2 \rho. \end{equation*}
Therefore
\begin{equation*} I = \frac{2\pi^2 \rho \lambda f^3 A^2}{r^2}. \end{equation*}
Replace \(\lambda\) by \(2\pi/k\) and \(f\) by \(\omega/2\pi\) to get
\begin{equation*} I = \pi \rho \frac{\omega^3}{2k} \frac{A^2}{r^2}. \end{equation*}
Solving for \(A\) we get
\begin{align*} A \amp = \sqrt{ \frac{2 k r^2 I}{\pi \rho\omega^3} } \\ \amp = \sqrt{ \frac{2\times 10 \times 0.25^2 \times 120}{\pi\times 1050\times 200^3} } = 7.54\times 10^{-5}\text{ m}^2. \end{align*}
