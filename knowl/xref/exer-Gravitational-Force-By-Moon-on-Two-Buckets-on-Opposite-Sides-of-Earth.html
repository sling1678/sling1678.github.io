Exercise 4. Gravitational Force By Moon on Two Buckets on Opposite Sides of Earth.

(a) Find the gravitational force of the Moon on a bucket of water of mass 1 kg placed at two sides of the Earth, one facing the Moon and the other exactly on the opposite side. (b) Think of a way to use your calculations to explain why there are two tides per day.
Solution.
Let’s frist draw a simple diagram of the situation. This is shown in Figure 12.16.
When the bucket is at the point \(B_1\) the distance from the center of the moon is \(r-R_E\) and when it is at the point \(B_2\text{,}\) the distance is \(r+R_E\text{.}\) The direction of the force on the bucket is towards the center of the moon in the two cases, and the magnitudes of the forces are
\begin{align*} \amp F_1 = G_N \frac{M_{\textrm{moon}}m}{(r-R_E)^2}.\\ \amp F_2 = G_N \frac{M_{\textrm{moon}}m}{(r+R_E)^2}. \end{align*}
Since \(R_E \ll r\text{,}\) we can expand these in terms of \(R_E/r\) and keep the first two terms in the expansions.
\begin{align*} \amp F_1 \approx G_N \frac{M_{\textrm{moon}}m}{r^2}\left[ 1 + 2\frac{R_E}{r} \right].\\ \amp F_2 \approx G_N \frac{M_{\textrm{moon}}m}{r^2}\left[ 1 - 2\frac{R_E}{r} \right]. \end{align*}
The difference in the magnitudes of the forces on the bucket at two locations can be found by just subtracting the magnitudes since they are in the same direction.
\begin{equation*} F_1 - F_2 \approx G_N \frac{M_{\textrm{moon}}m}{r^2}\times \frac{4R_E}{r}. \end{equation*}
Now, you can put the numerical values to obtain the numerical answer. The bucket has a larger force when at \(B_1\) compared to when at \(B_2\text{.}\) These tidal force causes the water level to be high at both \(B_1\) and \(B_2\text{.}\)
