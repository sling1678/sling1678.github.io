Exercise 7. Electric Field in two Wires Welded Together and Carrying Current.

Two rectangular cross-section wires of copper and aluminum are welded together seamlessly. A steady current of \(50\, \text{A}\) is passed though them. The areas of cross-sections of the wires are \(4\, \text{mm}^2\) each. (a) What is the value and direction of the electric field in each wire? Assume uniform electric field. (b) What excess charges are responsible for the difference in electric field in the two materials, and where are they located?
Density of copper = \(8000\, \text{kg/m}^3\text{;}\) Density of aluminum = \(2700\, \text{kg/m}^3\text{;}\) Atomic weight of copper = 65; Atomic weight of aluminum = 27; Conductivity of copper = \(5.9 \times 10^7\, \Omega^{-1}\text{m}^{-1}\text{;}\) Conductivity of aluminum = \(3.7 \times 10^7\, \Omega^{-1}\text{m}^{-1}\text{.}\)
Answer.
(a) \(\text{Cu}\, 0.21\, \text{V/m}\text{,}\) \(\text{Al}\, 0.34\, \text{V/m}\text{.}\) (b) \(1.15\times 10^{-12}\ \textrm{C/m}^2\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
Note the current density is same in the two wires since equal current flows past equal cross-sectional area. The magnitude of the volume current density is
\begin{equation*} J = \dfrac{50\:\textrm{A}}{4\:\textrm{mm}^2} \times \dfrac{10^6\:\textrm{mm}^2}{1\textrm{m}^2}= 1.25\times 10^{7}\:\textrm{A/m}^2. \end{equation*}
\noindent (a) Electric field is in the direction of current. The magnitude of electric field in the two metals are different because their conductivities are different according to Ohmâ€™s law.
\begin{equation*} E = \dfrac{J}{\sigma}. \end{equation*}
Therefore electric field in Copper and Aluminum are
\begin{align*} \amp E_{\textrm{in Cu}} = \dfrac{1.25\times 10^7\:\textrm{A/m}^2}{5.9\times 10^7\:\Omega^{-1}\textrm{m}^{-1}} = 0.21\:\textrm{V/m}.\\ \amp E_{\textrm{in Al}} = \dfrac{1.25\times 10^7\:\textrm{A/m}^2}{3.7\times 10^7\:\Omega^{-1}\textrm{m}^{-1}} = 0.34\:\textrm{V/m}. \end{align*}
Solution 2. b
Imagine a box-shaped Gaussian surface straddling the interface. Let the charge collected at the interface be denoted by \(\sigma_{\textrm{face}}\text{.}\) Then we see that there must be excess charges accumulated at the interface.
\begin{equation*} \left( E_{\textrm{in Al}} - E_{\textrm{in Cu}} \right) = \dfrac{\sigma_{\textrm{face}}\:A}{\epsilon_0}, \end{equation*}
where \(A\) is the area of cross-section of the Gaussian box. \noindent Hence, the charge density at the interface is
\begin{equation*} \sigma_{\textrm{face}} = \epsilon_0\: \left( E_{\textrm{in Al}} - E_{\textrm{in Cu}} \right) = 8.85\times 10^{-12}\times (0.34-0.21) = 1.15\times 10^{-12}\:\textrm{C/m}^2. \end{equation*}
