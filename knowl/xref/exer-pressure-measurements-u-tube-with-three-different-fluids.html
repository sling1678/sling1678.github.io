Exercise 4. U-tube with Three Different Fluids.

Ethyl alcohol is now poured on the other side of the mercury in ExerciseÂ 17.4.4.3. How much ethyl alcohol must be poured so that the water level on the other side rises by 1 cm?
Solution.
This would be a three-liquid problem. The figure here shows the situation with the three-liquids in the tube where \(h_3\) is the height of the alcohol. The only liquid with the same liquid on both sides is mercury.
Therefore, we take two points in the mercury, one in the left tube and the other on the right tube that is at the same vertical height as done in the last problem. With \(\rho_3\) denoting the density of the alcohol used in the experiment equating pressure at the same level of mercury on the two sides gives
\begin{equation*} \rho_1 (h_1-h) = \rho_2 (h_2-h) + \rho_3 h_3. \end{equation*}
The heights \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) have been determined in the last problem. Here \(h\) is given to be 1 cm.
\begin{equation*} h_1 = 27.2 \ \textrm{cm};\ \ h_2 = 2\ \textrm{cm};\ \ h = 1\ \textrm{cm} \end{equation*}
The densities are
\begin{equation*} \rho_1 = 1.0 \ \textrm{g/cm}^3;\ \ \rho_2 = 13.6 \ \textrm{g/cm}^3;\ \ \rho_3 = 0.81 \ \textrm{g/cm}^3. \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} h_3 = 15.6\ \textrm{cm}. \end{equation*}
