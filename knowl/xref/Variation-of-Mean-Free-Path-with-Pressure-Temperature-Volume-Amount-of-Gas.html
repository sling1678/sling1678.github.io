Exercise 2. Variation of Mean Free Path with Pressure, Temperature, Volume, Amount of Gas.

What will happen to the mean free path in ExerciseÂ 27.3.1 if we do the following:
(a) double the pressure while keeping the other quantities same?
(b) double the temperature while keeping the other quantities same?
(c) double the volume while keeping the other quantities same?
(d) double the amount of gas, i.e. mass, while keeping the other quantities same?
(e) double the mass and volume while keeping the other quantities same?
Hint.
Think proportionality relations, e.g., for (a) \(\lambda \sim 1/p\text{.}\)
Answer.
See the solutions.
Solution 1. (a)
Since \(\lambda \sim 1/p\text{,}\) doubling \(p\) would halve \(\lambda\text{.}\)
Solution 2. (b)
Since \(\lambda \sim T\text{,}\) doubling the temperature in the Absolute scale \(T\) would make \(\lambda\) twice as large. This makes sense since the speed of molecules will go up.
Solution 3. (c)
Doubling the volume will double the mean-free path.
Solution 4. (d)
If you increase the number of molecules of the gas in the same volume, the mean-free path will decrease in proportion since \(\lambda \sim 1/N\text{,}\) where \(N\) is the number of molecules.
Solution 5. (e)
The mean-free path is proportional to the ratio of \(V\) to \(N\text{,}\) so if you doubled both, \(\lambda\) would not change.
