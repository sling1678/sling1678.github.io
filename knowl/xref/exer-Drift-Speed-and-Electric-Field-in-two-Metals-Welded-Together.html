Exercise 6. Drift Speed and Electric Field in two Metals Welded Together.

One end of Gauge 6 (diameter \(0.162\, \text{in}\)) copper wire is welded to a Gauge 6 aluminum wire. A current of \(10\, \text{A}\) passes through the composite wire. (a) Find the current density in each wire. (b) Assuming there is one conduction electron per atom for copper and one conduction electron per atom for aluminum, find the drift velocity of electrons in copper and in aluminum for the current given. (c) Why is the drift velocity in the copper wire greater than that in the aluminum wire? (d) Why do the drift velocities much smaller than common everyday experience of almost instantaneous effect of turning electrical appliances when switch is thrown?
Answer.
(a) \(7.5\times 10^5\ \textrm{A/m}^2\text{,}\) (b) \(\text{Cu } 6.03\times 10^{-5}\ \textrm{m/s}\text{,}\) \(\text{Al } 7.78\times 10^{-5}\ \textrm{m/s}\text{.}\)
Solution 1. a
Let us express the radius of the wire in the SI units.
\begin{equation*} R = \dfrac{0.162\:\textrm{in}}{2}\times \dfrac{0.0254\:\textrm{m}}{\textrm{in}} = 2.06\times 10^{-3}\:\textrm{m}. \end{equation*}
Hence, the average volume current density would be
\begin{equation*} J = \dfrac{I}{\pi R^2} = \dfrac{10\:\textrm{A}}{\pi ( 2.06\times 10^{-3}\:\textrm{m})^2} = 7.50\times 10^5\:\textrm{A/m}^2. \end{equation*}
Solution 2. b
The volume current density in a metal is also equal to the product of the charge density of conducting electrons and their drift speed. Hence, drift speed can be evaluated from current density found in (a) and charge density of conducting electrons, which will be different for the two metals.
\begin{equation*} v_d = \dfrac{J}{\rho_{\textrm{conducting}}}. \end{equation*}
Let us work out the charge density of conducting electrons in Copper.
\begin{align*} \rho_{\textrm{Cu}} \amp= \dfrac{\textrm{No. atoms}}{\textrm{m}^3}\times 1\:\textrm{e} \\ \amp = \dfrac{6.022\times 10^{23}\:\textrm{atoms}}{0.064\:\textrm{kg}} \times \dfrac{8000\:\textrm{kg}}{\textrm{m}^3} \times 1.6\times 10^{-19}\:\textrm{C} = 1.20\times 10^{10}\:\textrm{C/m}^3. \end{align*}
Hence, the drift speed of conducting electrons in copper is
\begin{equation*} (v_d)_{\textrm{Cu}} = \dfrac{J}{\rho_{\textrm{Cu}}} = \dfrac{7.5\times 10^{5}\:\textrm{A/m}^2}{1.20\times 10^{10}\:\textrm{C/m}^3} = 6.03\times 10^{-5}\:\textrm{m/s}. \end{equation*}
A similar calculation, using the density \(2700\, \text{kg/m}^3\) and atomic weight \(0.027\, \text{kg/mole}\) leads to the following drift speed in aluminum.
\begin{equation*} (v_d)_{\textrm{Al}} = 7.78\times 10^{-5}\:\textrm{m/s}. \end{equation*}
Solution 3. c
Although, the electrons move at drift speed, an electron moving out from any point of the wire is replaced by another electron entering at that point. The signal itself moves at the speed of light.
