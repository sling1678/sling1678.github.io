Exercise 3. Wave Properties of a String Vibrating in First Harmonic.

A string of length 120 cm, density 20 g/m, and tension 20 N, is vibrating in its first harmonic with maximum displacement of \(1/2\) cm at any point of the string. (a) Where is/are the anti-node(s), and what is the value of amplitude at an antinode? (b) What is the value of the amplitude of oscillations at the following points from one end of the string: (i) 10 cm, (ii) 20 cm, (iii) 40 cm, and (iv) 60 cm? (c) How much time do the particles in (b) take to go from maximum amplitude to zero amplitude?
Solution 1. a
The vibration profile of the first overtone has the shape of one wavelength of the wave. This gives the nodes at the ends and in the middle and the anti-nodes at 1/3-rd of the way from either end. The amplitude at the antinodes will be the equal to the maximum amplitude anywhere in the string, i.e. 1/2 cm.
Solution 2. b
The mode at the instant when the string is furthest from the horizontal position at the anti-nodes can be fit with the sine function \(A(x) = (0.5\ \text{cm}) \sin(2\pi x/L)\) with \(x=0\) at one end and \(x=L = 120\ \text{cm}\) at the other end. By plugging in various values of \(x\) we can find the amplitude of the vibrations at that point.
  1. For \(x = 10\) cm, we get \(A = (0.5\ \text{cm}) \sin(2\pi 10\ \text{cm}/120\ \text{cm}) = 0.25\) cm.
  2. For \(x = 20\) cm, we get \(A = (0.5\ \text{cm}) \sin(2\pi 20\ \text{cm}/120\ \text{cm}) = 0.433\) cm
  3. For \(x = 40\) cm, we get \(A = (0.5\ \text{cm}) \sin(2\pi 40\ \text{cm}/120\ \text{cm}) = 0.433\) cm.
  4. For \(x = 60\) cm, we get \(A = (0.5\ \text{cm}) \sin(2\pi 60\ \text{cm}/120\ \text{cm}) = 0\) cm.
Solution 3. c
The time required is the time for quarter of a cycle, each cycle being completed in one period. Therefore
\begin{equation*} \Delta t = \frac{T}{4} = \frac{1}{4f}. \end{equation*}
The frequency of this mode will be related to the wavelength and the speed.
\begin{equation*} f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{L} \sqrt{T/\mu} = \frac{1}{1.20\ \text{m}} \sqrt{20\ \text{N}/0.020\ \text{kg/m}} = 26.4\ \text{Hz}. \end{equation*}
Therefore, the time will be
\begin{equation*} \Delta t= \frac{1}{4f} = \frac{1}{4\times26.4\ \text{Hz}} = 9.47\ \text{msec}. \end{equation*}
