Exercise 58. Power of a Biker to Go Uphill.
A cyclist in a race must climb a \(5^{\circ}\) hill at a speed of \(8 \text{ m/s}\) (see Figure 8.72). The mass of the bike and the biker together is \(80 \text{ kg}\text{.}\) In addition of providing the increase in his potential energy, he must also overcome air drag. Suppose air drag takes away energy at \(200\text{ J/s}\) on that speed. What must be the power output of the biker to achieve the goal?
Hint.
Find energy needed in an interval \(\Delta t\text{.}\)
Answer.
\(747 \text{ W} \text{.}\)
Solution.
We examine the work and energy picture during an interval \(t\) to \(t+\Delta t\text{.}\) During this time, the biker will go a distance \(d = v \Delta t\) and his height will increase by \(h = d\,\sin\,\theta\) as illustrated in Figure 8.73.
The biker must provide the energy equal to the change in his and bike’s potential energy and the dissipation of energy through air drag.
\begin{equation*} E_\text{provided} = mgh + 200\,\Delta t. \end{equation*}
Here
\begin{equation*} h = v\Delta t\,\sin\,\theta = 0.697\, \Delta t. \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} E_\text{provided}= 80\times 9.81\times 0.697\,\Delta t + 200\,\Delta t = 747\,\Delta t. \end{equation*}
Dividing by the duration will give us the power output of the biker.
\begin{equation*} P = 747\text{ W}. \end{equation*}
