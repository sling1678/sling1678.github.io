Suppose the spring is oriented along \(x\)-axis and you pull the block of mass \(m\) by a distance \(x_0\) from its equilibrium and give it a push so that its starting speed is \(v_0\text{,}\) we will show below that the resulting motion is a sinusoidally oscillatory motion with \(x\) between \(x=-A\) and \(x=+A\) with
\begin{equation} A = \sqrt{ x_0^2 + \left( v_0/\omega \right)^2 },\tag{13.3} \end{equation}
with period
\begin{equation} T = 2\pi \sqrt{ \dfrac{m}{k} },\tag{13.4} \end{equation}
and frequency
\begin{equation} f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{ \dfrac{k}{m} }.\tag{13.5} \end{equation}
Frequency tells us the number of repeat of the entire cycle of motion per unit of time. Often, we need to represent cycle of motion by a change of angle of a sine or cosine by \(2\pi\) radians. Then, we use what is called angular frequency, which is number of \(2\pi\) radians per unit time that such as funnction representing the motion undergoes. Angular frequency is usually denoted by \(\omega\text{.}\)
\begin{equation} \omega = 2\pi f = \sqrt{ \dfrac{k}{m} }.\tag{13.6} \end{equation}
Quantity \(A\) is called amplitude of the motion. Clearly, if you have a spring of known spring constant \(k\text{,}\) then you can find the mass of any object attached to it from measuring the period of the corresponding oscillations!
