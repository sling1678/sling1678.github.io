In practical applications of driven circuits, we work with \(R,\ L,\ C\) values corresponding to underdamped circuit. That is,
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{\gamma}{2} \lt \omega_0, \end{equation*}
where, as usual,
\begin{equation*} \gamma = \dfrac{R}{L},\ \ \ \ \omega_0 = \dfrac{1}{ \sqrt{LC} }. \end{equation*}
We will show below that, for \(t \gg 2/\gamma\text{,}\) current in the circuit will be
\begin{equation} I(t) = I_0\,\cos(\omega t + \phi_I),\tag{40.29} \end{equation}
where
\begin{align} \amp I_0 = \dfrac{V_0}{\sqrt{R^2+\left(\omega L - \dfrac{1}{\omega C} \right)^2}}. \tag{40.30}\\ \amp \phi_I = \dfrac{\pi}{2} + \tan^{-1} \left(\dfrac{1}{\omega R C} - \dfrac{\omega L}{R} \right). \tag{40.31} \end{align}
Here \(\phi_I\) is called phase constant of current. The argument of cosine for sinusoidal function is called phase. The phase of the driving EMF in \(V_0\cos(\omega t)\) is set to zero as reference. The phase constant \(\phi_I\) of current in the circuit is relative to the phase of the driving EMF. Note, to compare phases of two sinusoidal quantities, you need to express both of them either in sine or cosine since every sine can be written as a cosine by
\begin{equation*} \sin(\theta) = \cos(\theta - \pi/2). \end{equation*}
