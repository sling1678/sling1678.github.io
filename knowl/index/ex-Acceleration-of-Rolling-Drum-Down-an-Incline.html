Exercise 1. Acceleration of Rolling Drum Down an Incline.

Find the acceleration of a drum rolling downhill without slipping on an slope inclined at angle \(\phi\text{.}\)
Hint.
Set up two equations - one for CM translation and one for rotation about CM.
Answer.
\(\frac{2}{3}\ g\ \sin\phi\text{.}\)
Solution.
First we draw a figure and identify the dynamical variables that will be helpful (see Figure 9.133). For translation of the CM since the acceleration of the CM is along the incline it is best to use a Cartesian coordinates with \(x\)-axis along the incline. For the rotation, we can use angle that a marked line, shown dashed line on the cross-section of the drum, makes with the vertical to the incline.
From Figure 9.133, we find that the angle rotated by the marked line and the distance traveled by the CM are related if the drum rolls without slipping.
\begin{equation*} X_\text{cm} = R\theta. \end{equation*}
Therefore, the angular velocity is related to the x-velocity of the CM.
\begin{equation*} V_{\text{cm-}x} = R\omega. \end{equation*}
The \(x\)-component of acceleration of the CM is, therefore, related to angular acceleration \(\alpha\text{.}\)
\begin{equation} A_{\text{cm-}x} = R\alpha. \tag{9.113} \end{equation}
and the y-component of the acceleration of CM is zero. Since acceleration is only along \(x\)-direction, I will drop subscript \(x\) in Eq. (9.113) and write the following instead
\begin{equation} A_\text{cm} = R\alpha. \tag{9.114} \end{equation}
The forces acting on the drum are its weight \(W(=Mg)\text{,}\) normal force \(N\) and static friction \(F_s\text{.}\) Now, we will set up the dynamical equations for the translation of the CM and rotation about CM. These equations together with Eq. (9.114) will be sufficient to solve for acceleration of the CM. Translation of the CM:
\begin{align} \amp x\text{-component}:\ \ \ M\ g\ \sin\phi-F_s = MA_\text{cm}.\tag{9.115}\\ \amp y\text{-component}:\ \ \ N-M\ g\ \cos\phi = 0.\tag{9.116} \end{align}
Rotation about the CM:
\begin{equation} F_sR = \frac{1}{2}MR^2\alpha. \tag{9.117} \end{equation}
Note that the torques by \(W\) and \(N\) about an axis through the CM are zero. Now, from Eqs. (9.114) and (9.117) we find \(F_s\) in terms of \(A_\text{cm}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation} F_s = \frac{1}{2}MA_\text{cm}\alpha. \tag{9.118} \end{equation}
Substituting \(F_s\) from Eq. (9.118) into Eq. (9.115) and solving for \(A_\text{cm}\) we find
\begin{equation*} A_\text{cm} = \frac{2}{3}\ g\ \sin\phi. \end{equation*}
