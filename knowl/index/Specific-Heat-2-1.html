The amount of heat that can change the temperature of a body by one degree is called the heat capacity of the body. Therefore, if heat \(Q\) raises the temperature by \(\Delta T\) then the heat capacity would be
\begin{equation} \text{Heat Capacity } = \dfrac{Q}{\Delta T}.\tag{22.1} \end{equation}
Heat capacity is a misleading term, since it seems to imply that heat may be contained in the system rather than having only a transitory nature. Although the term “heat capacity” is still in use in certain disciplines such as Chemistry, but in Physics we don’t use this term any more. Instead, physicists prefer a related concept called specific heat.
