Front face (refraction at the convex surface):
\begin{equation} \frac{n_1}{p_1} + \frac{n_2}{q_1} = \frac{n_2-n_1}{R_1},\ \ \ (q_1 \lt 0 \textrm{ and } R_1 \gt 0).\tag{44.27} \end{equation}
Back face (refraction at the concave surface):
\begin{equation} \frac{n_2}{p_2} + \frac{n_1}{q_2} = \frac{n_1-n_2}{R_2},\ \ \ \left(p_2 \gt 0, q_2 \gt 0, R_2 \lt 0 \right).\tag{44.28} \end{equation}
We have the following additional relation. The object distance for the second interface is from \(\text{V}_2\) to \(\text{Q}_1\text{,}\) and since \(q_1\) is negative, i.e., \(q_1 \lt 0\text{,}\) the distance \(p_2\) will depend on \(q_1\) and the width \(d\) of the lens.
\begin{equation} p_2 = |q_1| + d = - q_1 + d.\tag{44.29} \end{equation}
Note that \(q_1\) is a negative number here, therefore \(-q_1\) is positive. Put \(p_2\) from Eq. (44.29)into Eq. (44.28) and add Eq. (44.27) and Eq. (44.28) to obtain the following.
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{p_1} + \frac{1}{q_2} = \left( \frac{n_2}{n_1} - 1 \right) \left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} \right) + \frac{n_2}{n_1}\ \frac{d}{q_1(q_1-d)}.\tag{44.30} \end{equation}
In the \(d \rightarrow 0\) limit, which is also called the thin lens approximation, the last term goes to zero, and \(\text{V}_1\) and \(\text{V}_2\) become one point. All distances are then measured from the center of the lens rather than either vertex. We can drop the subscripts on \(p\) and \(q\) since the distances are measured from the center.
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = \left( \frac{n_2}{n_1} - 1 \right) \left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} \right).\tag{44.31} \end{equation}
This equation is called the lens-maker equation. Identifying the right side as inverse of focal length \(1/f\text{,}\) i.e, power of the lens, we get the famous lens equation.
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = \frac{1}{f},\tag{44.32} \end{equation}
with
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{f} = \left( \frac{n_2}{n_1} - 1 \right) \left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} \right).\tag{44.33} \end{equation}
This formula for power of the lens simplifies if medium around lens is air. In that case, we set \(n_1=1\) and \(n_2 = n_l\text{.}\) This simplified formula for focal length of a thin lens is called lens-maker formula.
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{f} = \left( n_l - 1 \right) \left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} \right).\tag{44.34} \end{equation}
Since \(n_l \gt 1\text{,}\) the sign of the focal length depends on the sign of the result of calculation of quantities in the parenthesis. Thus, for bi-convex lens (\(R_1 \gt 0\text{,}\) \(R_2 \lt 0\)), you will always get postive \(f\) and for bi-concave lens (\(R_1 \lt 0\text{,}\) \(R_2 \gt 0\)), you will always get negative \(f\text{.}\) For some other mixed curvatures the sign of \(f\) will depend on the actual values of curvatures. If \(f\) is positive we call the lens converging lens and if negative diverging lens.
