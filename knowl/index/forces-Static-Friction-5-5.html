The ratio of \(F_s^{\text{max}} \) to the magnitude of the normal force \(F_N \) for a surface turns out to be a characteristic of the surface of contact. We call this ratio the coefficient of static friction and denote by the Greek letter \(\mu_s\) (read mu sub s).
\begin{equation} \mu_s = \dfrac{F_s^{\text{max}}}{F_N}.\tag{6.16} \end{equation}
If two surfaces are stuck together, then they cannot slide past each other. In that case \(\mu_s = \infty \text{,}\) and if you donâ€™t need any force to slide, i.e., for a frictionless surface, \(\mu_s = 0 \text{.}\) That is, \(0 \le \mu_s \lt \infty\text{.}\)
