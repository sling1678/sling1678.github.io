Remark 14.25. Decibels.

Often we need to compare intensities of two waves. Since intensities vary over a wide range, comparison is usually done using logarithm since taking a log of a number compresses the number. When we compare two intensities by using base 10 log we call the log of their ratios a bel after Alexander Graham Bell, the inventor of telephone. The decibel is a factor of 10 of a bel as is the case with deci-meter and deci-gram, etc. Decibel is denoted by the symbol dB. Thus, decibel value of an intensity \(I\) compared to a reference intensity \(I_0\) is given by
\begin{equation} \text{dB} = 10\,\log_{10} \left( \frac{I}{I_0} \right)\tag{14.62} \end{equation}
Since taking the ratio of two intensities cancels out units, decibel does not have any dimensions - itâ€™s a pure number. Reference intensities may be chosen for different waves and for different purposes. For instance, the reference intensity for sound has the following value
\begin{equation*} \left( I_0 \right)_\text{sound} = 1.0 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{W/m}^2. \end{equation*}
This is the lowest intensity of sound that an adult of normal hearing can perceive. Then, the intensity of a sound of \(20\,\text{dB}\) can be determined to be
\begin{equation*} 20\,\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10}\left( \frac{I}{I_0} \right)\longrightarrow I = 10^{2} \times I_0 = 1.0 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{W/m}^2. \end{equation*}
