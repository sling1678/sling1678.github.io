Example 6.112. Conical Pendulum.

A pendulum of length \(l=1.2\text{ m}\) and mass \(0.4\text{ kg}\) is swung not in a vertical plane but on the surface of a cone. The bob goes in a circle at constant speed of \(3.5\text{ m/s}\) when the string makes an angle \(\theta\) with the vertical axis. At different speeds you get different angles. This is called conical pendulum.
Find angle \(\theta\text{,}\) centripetal acceleration, tangential acceleration and tension in the string.
Answer.
\(a_c = 12.85\text{ m/s}^2\text{,}\) \(a_T=0\text{,}\) \(\theta = 52.6^\circ\text{,}\) \(T=6.46\text{ N}\text{.}\)
Solution.
Let us draw forces and acceleration directions. Since speed is constant, tangential acceleration is zero. We also see that circle is in horizontal plane, which makes centripetal force in the horizontal plane. With gravity vertically down, gravity will have no component towards acceleration.
Therefore, we get the following equations of motion along the centripetal and vertical directions. Note: the tangential direction to circle just gives \(0=0\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} \amp T\sin\theta = m a_c = m \frac{v^2}{l\sin\theta}\\ \amp T\cos\theta - mg = 0 \end{align*}
From these equations we get
\begin{equation*} \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{v^2}{gl}. \end{equation*}
Let \(x = \cos\theta \text{.}\) This gives a quadratic equation.
\begin{equation*} x^2 + b x - 1 = 0, \ \ \ b=\frac{v^2}{gl} \end{equation*}
This gives \(x=\cos\theta\) to be
\begin{equation*} \cos\theta = \frac{1}{2}\left[-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 + 4} \right]. \end{equation*}
Using the numerical values we get
\begin{equation*} \cos\theta = 0.607, -1.7. \end{equation*}
Clearly \(\cos\theta = -1.7\) is unphysical. So, we get
\begin{equation*} \theta = \cos^{-1}(0.607) = 52.6^\circ. \end{equation*}
Using this value, we get centripetal acceleration and tension to be
\begin{align*} \amp a_c = \frac{v^2}{l\sin\theta} = 12.85\text{ m/s}^2.\\ \amp T = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta} = 6.46\text{ N}. \end{align*}
