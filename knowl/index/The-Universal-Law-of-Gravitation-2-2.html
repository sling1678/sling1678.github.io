The magnitude of the gravitational force \(F\) between two particles of masses \(m_1 \) and \(m_2 \) separated by a distance \(r \) is given by
\begin{equation} F = G_N\, \dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2},\tag{12.10} \end{equation}
where \(G_N\) is a universal constant, called gravitational constant. Do not confuse this \(G_N\) with acceleration due to gravity \(g\text{,}\) although sometimes we write \(G\) for \(G_N\text{.}\) The value of \(G_N\) has been determined experimentally to be
\begin{equation} G_N = 6.67428\times10^{-11}\ \text{N.(m/kg)}^2.\tag{12.11} \end{equation}
Constant \(G_N\) is considered to be truly universal, being same between any two objects anywhere in the universe. However, the great English physicist Paul A. M. Dirac has hypothesized that the value of \(G_N\) may have been different in the past (see Dirac

). A non-constant \(G_N\) will have significant cosmological consequences, which is not fully understood yet.
